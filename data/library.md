## [010] Your AI Budget Is Growing. Your Returns Aren't. Here's Why.

| Field | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | Michael Heric, Purna Doddapaneni, and Antoine Debarre |
| **Source** | Bain & Company (Brief) |
| **Published** | June 1, 2026 |
| **Added** | June 29, 2026 |
| **Type** | Survey-based analysis / industry brief |
| **URL** | https://www.bain.com/insights/your-ai-budget-is-growing-your-returns-arent-heres-why/ |
| **Themes** | AI tooling, AI guardrails / policy, data & targeting, case study |

**Summary.** Drawing on Bain's Automation and AI Pathfinder Survey of 951 global companies, the piece argues that AI ROI shortfalls are organizational, not technological. Nearly 40% of companies that measured AI cost savings landed below 10%, despite targeting 11% to 20%, yet 90% are increasing their budgets again. The authors identify three structural problems: investment cases assume full-automation economics that don't match the human-in-the-loop reality most agents actually run in; the next wave of AI spending is largely self-funded from prior savings that didn't fully materialize; and data access remains the top-cited barrier to progress — notably more so for companies that hit their targets than for those that missed them, since high performers are running into the problem harder by deploying at greater scale. The piece closes with five prescriptive moves for leaders: fix workflow debt before automating it, validate the investment case and name a governance owner up front, use AI to work around imperfect data rather than waiting for clean data, redesign the operating model around human-agent collaboration, and measure outcomes at the enterprise level rather than the program level.

**Key takeaways.**
- Only 7% of companies are running fully autonomous agents in production; 38% require human approval and 32% operate with guardrails and exceptions. The gap between the investment case (often modeled on full automation) and the operating reality (a human-approval queue) is itself a cost the business case didn't anticipate.
- 44% of companies are funding their next AI wave from savings generated by prior automation programs — savings that, per the survey's own headline finding, came in well below target for a large share of respondents.
- Data access and integration is the single biggest barrier to AI progress, cited by 41% of respondents, ahead of compliance, budget, skills, and executive buy-in.
- Accountability for agent decisions is currently diffuse, with governance split roughly evenly between IT, business functions, and central teams — a gap that must be resolved before deployment, not after an incident.

**Notable data points.**
- Survey base: 951 global companies (Bain Automation and AI Pathfinder Survey 2026).
- ~40% of companies measuring cost savings landed in the 0–10% range versus a targeted 11–20%.
- 7% fully autonomous agents in production; 38% human-approval-required; 32% guardrails-with-exceptions.
- 44% cite prior automation savings as their primary funding source for GenAI/agentic AI investment.
- Data barrier cited by 44% of on-target companies vs. 40% of off-target companies.

**Relevance to the project.**
- Directly useful for the WTE engagement's Phase 1: the autonomy spectrum (human approval required / guardrails with exceptions) is a good external benchmark for where WTE's current pilots sit, and gives language for why staying human-in-the-loop right now is the right posture.
- The "workflow debt" argument maps cleanly onto the discovery-before-prescription discipline already established for this engagement.
- The governance-ownership gap is a useful prompt for a concrete deliverable in the baseline assessment: naming who at WTE is accountable if an AI-assisted output goes out wrong.

**Possible uses.**
- Citable third-party data point for the 2028 playbook section on sequencing and pacing AI investment.
- Could anchor a slide in the WTE baseline-assessment readout: "most orgs aren't running autonomous agents — they're running human-approval queues, and the business case should say so."
- Useful precedent-setting language for a WTE AI governance/accountability one-pager.

## [011] Vibe Code Check: 300+ Marketing Leaders on How AI Code Generation Is Empowering Their Teams

| Field | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | UserEvidence, in partnership with chiefmartec (Scott Brinker) |
| **Source** | UserEvidence (survey report, sponsored/co-branded research) |
| **Published** | June 2026 |
| **Added** | June 29, 2026 |
| **Type** | Survey-based industry report |
| **URL** | https://userevidence.com/report/vibe-coding-marketing-report/ |
| **Themes** | AI tooling, AI guardrails / policy, data & targeting, investment / funding, case study |

**Summary.** A survey of 302 SaaS marketing leaders, managers, and practitioners on adoption of "vibe coding" — using AI code-generation tools to build internal apps, automations, and integrations without waiting on engineering. The headline finding is that adoption has gone mainstream fast: most marketing orgs surveyed already vibe code with at least some structure, despite the practice and terminology being barely a year old. Use is concentrated in internal/operational tooling rather than customer-facing products. The report's strongest argument is that tool choice matters less than organizational practice — specifically, who introduces the practice (practitioner-led beats IT-led beats vendor-led for speed to ROI), whether governance is formalized early, and whether training is treated as a real guardrail rather than an afterthought.

**Key takeaways.**
- Four out of 10 (41%) respondents say vibe coding is widely used or fully embedded in team workflows; only 7% are still at the single-experimenter stage. Over half (57%) have been using these tools for less than a year.
- Confidence growth plateaus through the 1–2 year mark and then jumps sharply at the 2-year mark, where 64% report high confidence — a "trough of disillusionment" pattern.
- 76% of respondents say their team's vibe-coding ability has changed hiring decisions — more often by changing the skills prioritized than by reducing headcount.
- Practitioner-led (vs. IT-led vs. vendor-led) introduction of the practice strongly predicts speed to ROI.
- Training is the most neglected guardrail despite being one of the strongest predictors of confidence and fast ROI.

**Notable data points.**
- Survey base: 302 marketing leaders/practitioners; 55% at 500–1,999 employees, 26% at 2,000+.
- Top tools by usage: GitHub Copilot (59%), OpenAI Codex (55%), Claude Code (47%), Claude Cowork (38%); orgs use an average of three tools.
- 83% have some governance framework; 46% have a formal, organization-wide policy. Just 21% include training as a guardrail.
- Practitioner-led introduction → 47% fast ROI; IT/engineering-led → 30%; vendor/agency-led → 14%.
- ⚠️ UserEvidence/chiefmartec co-branded research; self-reported survey data from corporate marketing practitioners, not political campaigns. Label as adjacent-sector data when citing.

**Relevance to the project.**
- "Who introduces it matters more than which tool" finding is directly exportable to WTE Phase 1: practitioner-driven rollout is empirically associated with faster, more durable adoption.
- Training-as-neglected-guardrail finding reinforces a likely WTE Phase 1 recommendation.
- Internal-tools-first, customer-facing-last adoption sequence mirrors a sensible analog for WTE.

**Possible uses.**
- Supporting data point for the 2028 playbook's "build internal capacity first" sequencing argument, with explicit caveat that it's drawn from corporate marketing.
- Useful for a baseline-assessment conversation about who should lead WTE's internal AI rollout.

## [013] Artificial Intelligence in Political Consulting: Key Findings from a Survey and In-Depth Interviews with AAPC Members (May 2025)

| Field | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | 3D Strategic Research and Normington Petts (commissioned by AAPC) |
| **Source** | American Association of Political Consultants (theaapc.org) |
| **Published** | May 20, 2025 (survey fielded April 22–May 4, 2025) |
| **Added** | June 29, 2026 |
| **Type** | Primary survey data / slide deck |
| **URL** | https://theaapc.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/AAPCF-AI-AAPC-Member-Survey-Presentation-July-2025.pdf |
| **Themes** | AI tooling, AI guardrails / policy, situational awareness, data & targeting |

**Summary.** The primary AAPC member survey (n=200; 131 current + 61 lapsed members; ±6.8% margin of error) plus 15 in-depth interviews, run by 3D Strategic Research and Normington Petts. This is the original source behind the AAPC poll cited secondhand in CCI's "Two Possible Futures" piece (entry 012). 41% of respondents said AI's future impact on political campaigns and consulting would "fundamentally transform" the industry, while 54% said it would be a useful tool without fundamentally changing it. ChatGPT dominates tool usage at 76%. The ethics section tests specific hypothetical AI use cases and finds a sharp ethical cliff between "real but AI-assisted" content and fabricated content.

**Key takeaways.**
- A majority of political consultants use AI weekly, including one third who use it daily; use is geared heavily toward internal workflows, brainstorming, and research rather than production or voter targeting.
- GOP consultants and daily users are consistently more likely than Democratic consultants and infrequent users to rate AI's impact as positive — a persistent finding across nearly every cross-tab.
- Accuracy/reliability of AI-generated insights (62%) and ethical concerns like bias or misinformation (44%) are the most cited challenges.
- Fabricated voter testimonials presented as "typical supporters" are rated unethical by 63% even when disclosed, and 90% when not disclosed.
- "AI overhyped or misused by campaigns that don't understand it" ranks among consultants' top concerns — a nuance less commonly cited elsewhere.

**Notable data points.**
- Survey: n=200 AAPC members, fielded April 22–May 4, 2025; ±6.8% margin of error. Plus 15 in-depth interviews.
- Sample: 44% Democrat, 40% Republican, 16% independent/non-partisan; 61% owner/partner seniority; 46% with 20+ years' experience.
- AI's role in 2024 election: 9% "very significant," 43% "moderate," 43% "a minimal role."
- Tool usage: ChatGPT 76%, Gemini 28%, Canva Magic Tools 21%, Microsoft Copilot 19%, Adobe Firefly 11%, Claude 10%.
- Top current use cases: summarizing/analyzing large text volumes (39%), drafting proposals/internal materials (32%), brainstorming/ideation (27%).
- Effectiveness: 24% "very effective," 46% "somewhat effective," 14% "not effective."

**Relevance to the project.**
- Authoritative primary source behind a stat already cited secondhand in two places in the library (entry 012; project research report). Use this as the canonical citation going forward.
- The partisan-gap finding is a load-bearing data point for the 2028 playbook's competitive framing.
- The ethics-by-scenario section is a genuinely useful internal-training resource for WTE — could shape a practical AI-use guardrails document for Phase 1.
- Low current usage of AI for voter targeting/segmentation, despite high perceived future opportunity, suggests this remains a frontier use case across the industry.

**Possible uses.**
- Primary citation for any deck or memo discussing the AAPC poll split (54%/41%).
- Ethics-guardrails discussion prompt for WTE: the hypothetical-scenario ladder is a ready-made exercise for where WTE wants to draw its own lines.
- Baseline ("before") data point when pairing with entry 014 to show year-over-year change.

## [014] AI Use Among Political Consultants is Booming. Here's How.

| Field | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | Max Greenwood |
| **Source** | Campaigns & Elections |
| **Published** | April 17, 2026 |
| **Added** | June 29, 2026 |
| **Type** | News article reporting on primary survey data |
| **URL** | https://campaignsandelections.com/industry-news/ai-use-among-political-consultants-is-booming/ |
| **Themes** | AI tooling, AI guardrails / policy, situational awareness |

**Summary.** Reports on a newer AAPC-commissioned survey — fielded March 7–15, 2026 by 3D Strategic Research and Promark Research, based on responses from 213 AAPC members — distinct from the May 2025 wave in entry 013. The headline finding is a sharp year-over-year jump in usage: 83% of political professionals now use AI tools at least once a week, up from 59% a year ago, and 48% now use AI multiple times a day, a 57% increase over 2025 levels. The article also tracks a persistent and widening partisan gap, rising optimism about AI's role, continued majority support for some additional government regulation, and a growing but still modest share of clients actively asking about AI use.

**Key takeaways.**
- Weekly-plus AI usage rose from 59% to 83% year-over-year; daily-multiple-times usage rose 57% to reach 48% — a steeper adoption curve than the 2025 baseline showed.
- The partisan gap persists: 64% of Republican consultants use AI daily versus 49% of Democrats, though both parties' usage grew at roughly the same rate (~20–21%) year-over-year. The gap is a stock, not a flow, problem.
- Sentiment warming: share calling AI's recent role "mostly positive" rose from 24% to 37%, while the negative share held flat at 18%.
- Client-driven pressure is still modest: 56% of consultants report clients asking about AI use at all, and only 8% say clients are actively pushing them to use it.

**Notable data points.**
- Survey: n=213 AAPC members, fielded March 7–15, 2026, by 3D Strategic Research and Promark Research.
- Weekly+ usage: 83% (2026) vs. 59% (2025). Multiple-times-daily: 48% (2026), up 57% over 2025.
- Effectiveness: 35% "very effective" (2026) vs. 24% (2025).
- Daily AI use by party (2026): 64% Republican vs. 49% Democrat.
- 67% overall want at least some additional regulation; 82% of Democrats vs. 53% of Republicans.
- 56% report clients asking about AI use; only 8% report clients actively pushing for AI use.

**Relevance to the project.**
- Most useful item in the library for benchmarking WTE's baseline against the broader Democratic-consulting industry.
- The year-over-year comparison with entry 013 documents the rate of change in the industry — directly useful for projecting where the field will be by 2028.
- "Client-led demand is still modest" supports the operator-not-evangelist framing: there isn't strong outside pressure forcing the pace, which validates a measured, baseline-first approach.

**Possible uses.**
- Primary citable source for "industry adoption is accelerating sharply" language in stakeholder conversations or the 2028 playbook.
- Pairs with entry 013 for a two-point trend line (2025 → 2026).
- Note: ethics-of-disclosure findings are in entry 013 only — don't conflate when citing.

## [017] Who Should Govern California (According to AI)?

| Field | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | Run for Something (organizational byline; CampSight team) |
| **Source** | Run for Something Action Fund / Medium |
| **Published** | June 12, 2026 |
| **Added** | June 30, 2026 |
| **Type** | Case study / field report |
| **URL** | https://runforsomething.medium.com/who-should-govern-california-according-to-ai-c9756539bc8e |
| **Themes** | AI tooling, data & targeting, case study, agentic AI |

**Summary.** A field report on how Run for Something's CampSight tool tracked AI-generated narratives about four Democratic candidates in the 2026 California governor's race (Steyer, Porter, Becerra, Mahan) over the final weeks of the campaign. The core argument: AI chatbots have become a primary voter-information channel, but the narratives they produce don't mirror polls, fundraising, or earned media in predictable ways — they reflect a combination of source-citation patterns, contrast structure, and viability signals that campaigns have not yet learned to manage. CalMatters is the most-cited domain across all candidates; Reddit threads appear as part of the "authoritative source" stack AI uses, sometimes without the underlying article being referenced. The piece documents how AI narratives shifted materially in the final week as new earned media landed — meaning voters consulting AI at different points received meaningfully different briefings. Frames "Generative Engine Optimization" (GEO / Answer Engine Optimization) as an emerging campaign discipline.

**Key takeaways.**
- The AI information environment is rewired: a Californian asking ChatGPT about the governor's race is more likely to see a CalMatters summary or a Reddit thread than a Sacramento Bee or New York Times article. Campaigns optimizing for traditional media are optimizing for the old information environment.
- AI narrative rankings diverge from expected hierarchies: Steyer, despite having the field's largest war chest, was repeatedly ranked sixth on cost-of-living and other issues — AI doesn't weight campaign spending, it weights source density, contrast utility, and viability signals from polling.
- AI uses "contrast" as a structural device: Steve Hilton appeared in 62–75% of AI responses about each of the four Democrats, including queries unrelated to him, because contrast provides useful explanatory structure.
- AI narratives are not stable: in the final week, citation sources and rankings shifted significantly as new earned media landed — voters consulting AI days apart received different briefings.
- The same questions asked in different languages returned different results and different sources — a non-English-language voter information gap almost entirely unaddressed by current campaign strategy.

**Notable data points.**
- Hilton appeared in AI responses for Mahan (75%), Becerra (73%), Porter (67%), and Steyer (62%).
- Steyer ranked first overall more often than any other candidate but landed in the bottom two in nearly a third of queries — the field's most polarizing AI positioning.
- Katie Porter's citation set narrowed in the final week, dropping the NYT, Sacramento Bee, Harvard Political Review, and Emerson College Polling while Facebook and Instagram gained prominence.
- ⚠️ This piece is written by the CampSight team — a Run for Something Action Fund product being promoted here. Treat specific tool claims as vendor-adjacent; the narrative-tracking observations are more independently verifiable.

**Relevance to the project.**
- Most forward-looking, externally-facing item in the library — describes a voter-information dynamic campaigns will need to account for by 2028 far more than in 2026.
- GEO/Answer Engine Optimization concept is named in entry 016 as a practitioner-identified frontier use case; this is the most developed field report on what it looks like in practice. Entries 016 and 017 should be read together.
- "Optimizing for the old information environment" critique is a clean framing for a 2028 playbook argument: understanding how AI characterizes Pete Buttigieg and building an earned-media/web-presence strategy to shape those characterizations should be on the 2028 roadmap.
- Multilingual finding is a significant equity and reach issue for a 2028 presidential campaign with diverse-language voter contact needs.

**Possible uses.**
- CA governor's race as a concrete, recent case study in the 2028 playbook's section on AI's role in the voter information environment.
- The Steyer/war-chest finding ("AI doesn't care how much you spend") is a counterintuitive, citable claim for stakeholder conversations about why the 2028 information environment requires thinking beyond traditional media investment.
- The contrast-figure phenomenon is a concrete talking point for explaining to non-technical stakeholders why a campaign's AI narrative is partly outside their control.
